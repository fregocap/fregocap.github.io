    # NLA Evaluations and the Geometric Prior

    This post explores the convergence properties of non-linear analysis (NLA) in transformer models. We argue that the standard evaluation metrics often miss the underlying geometric prior that emerges during the mid-stages of training.

    ## The Problem Statement

    Consider a standard transformer layer defined by the transformation $f(x) = \text{Softmax}(QK^T)V$. Traditionally, we analyze the attention patterns as a discrete graph. However, as the model scales, the latent space $Z \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ begins to exhibit properties of a smooth manifold.

    We can define the information density $\rho$ as:

    $$ \rho(z) = \int_{\Omega} \frac{\exp(-\|z - \mu\|^2 / 2\sigma^2)}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} d\mu $$

    ## Emergent Structures

    As training progresses, the model minimizes the cross-entropy loss $\mathcal{L}$. Our research suggests that this minimization is equivalent to a regularized Ricci flow on the latent space manifold. 
    
    This has significant implications for how we perform evaluations. Instead of looking at top-1 accuracy, we should be measuring the curvature of the embedding space.

    ### Key Insights
    - **Manifold Collapse:** High learning rates lead to a collapse of the geometric prior.
    - **Entropy Regularization:** Natural language acts as a stochastic regularizer for $\sigma$.
  </div>

  <div class="sidenote">
    Note: We found that models trained with Adam optimizer exhibit faster manifold convergence but are prone to higher curvature instability.

    ## Conclusion

    The path forward for evaluating large language models lies in differential geometry. By treating the weights not as static matrices but as coordinate systems on a dynamic manifold, we can predict failure modes before they manifest in natural language output.
