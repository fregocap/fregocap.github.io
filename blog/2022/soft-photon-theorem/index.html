Soft Photon Theorem

A blog post about an argument put forward by Weinberg that explains why massless spin 1 bosons are associated with conserved charges and why massless spin 2 bosons interact like a graviton.

In order to follow the argument of Weinberg, we first need to introduce some prerequisites of physics. The first thing to know is that the photon is a massless spin 1 boson. Therefore, it only has two polarisations. It can never oscillate in the direction of travel because its speed would vary then and it constantly moves at the speed of light.

In other words, its polarisation vector $\epsilon$ is always orthogonal to its momentum vector $\mathbf{k}$, i.e.:

\[\epsilon \cdot \mathbf{k} = 0\]

In addition, we want the photon to be massless $\mathbf{k}^2 = 0$. Remember that $\mathbf{k}$ are 4-vectors , and $\mathbf{k}^2=m^2$ is the invariant mass of the particle. Therefore, shifting the polarisation vector by something proportional to $\mathbf{k}$ can then never change the physics:

\[\begin{eqnarray} \epsilon &&\rightarrow \epsilon + a\mathbf{k} \nonumber, \\ \epsilon \cdot \mathbf{k} &&\rightarrow \epsilon \cdot \mathbf{k}+ a\mathbf{k}^2 = \epsilon \cdot \mathbf{k} \nonumber \end{eqnarray}\]

A massless spin 2 particle is represented by a symmetric, traceless matrix. Its polarisation tensor is orthogonal to its momentum as well, again because it travels with the speed of light. The important difference to a spin 1 particle is that $\epsilon$ is a matrix, not a vector.

The Soft Photon

Imagine now the same process, but in addition a spin 1 particle with a very low momentum $\mathbf{k}$ (soft) is produced. We denote the interaction strength between the spin 1 boson and the ith particle by $g_i$.

We now want to express the second amplitude as a term multiplying the first amplitude. More precisely, we want the largest contribution to this term in the limit $\mathbf{k}\rightarrow 0$. The spin 1 particle has a polarisation vector, but the amplitude is a scalar. So, we need another vector to build a scalar product with $\epsilon$. We only have momentum vectors and if $\mathbf{k}$ is small, $\mathbf{p}_i$ is large, so the largest term should scale like $\mathbf{p}_i \cdot \epsilon$.

Moreover, there must be a denominator (for dimensional reasons alone). The smallest denominator, for the largest possible contribution in the limit $\mathbf{k} \rightarrow 0$ scales like $\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{p}_i$. One can derive that term from the internal propagator using $\mathbf{k}^2=0$ and $\mathbf{p}_i^2=m_i^2$:

\[\frac{1}{(\mathbf{p}_i+\mathbf{k})^2-m_i^2} = \frac{1}{\mathbf{p}^2_i+\mathbf{k}^2+2\mathbf{p}_i\cdot\mathbf{k} - m_i^2}= \frac{1}{2 \mathbf{p}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}}\]

If we sum over all possible legs, the spin 1 particle can be radiated off from, we get the expression:

\[\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{p}_1,\cdots,\mathbf{p}_n,\mathbf{k}) = \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{p}_1,\cdots,\mathbf{p}_n) \times \sum_i {\color{red}{g_i}}\frac{\epsilon\cdot\mathbf{p}_i}{2\mathbf{p}_i\cdot \mathbf{k}}\]

Now comes the crucial step: we know the physics do not change if we shift $\epsilon\rightarrow \epsilon +a\mathbf{k}$. That means that the term in which the $a\mathbf{k}$ appears cannot contribute at all. We can work out the consequences of that by replacing $\epsilon \rightarrow a\mathbf{k}$ above and demand:

\[\mathcal{A}\times \sum_i {\color{red}{g_i}}\frac{a\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{p}_i}{2\mathbf{p}_i\cdot \mathbf{k}} = \mathcal{A} \times a \sum_i {\color{red}{g_i}} = 0.\]

It vanishes, but only if the sum over all values of couplings $g_i$ between the spin 1 boson and the i particles vanish: this is charge conservation ! A consisting interacting massless spin 1 particle automatically requires charge conservation.

The Graviton

What changes if we consider a spin 2 boson instead ? The steps of the calculation are exactly the same, but now one needs 2 momentum vectors in the numerator, because the polarisations of a spin 2 particle are described by a matrix. The largest term is:

\[\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{p}_1,\cdots,\mathbf{p}_n, \mathbf{k}) = \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{p}_1,\cdots,\mathbf{p}_n) \sum_i {\color{red}{g_i}} \frac{\mathbf{p}_i \epsilon \mathbf{p}_i}{2 \mathbf{k} \mathbf{p}_i}\]

Again, nothing may change under the shift $\epsilon \rightarrow \epsilon +a \mathbf{k}$ and replacing $\epsilon$ by $a\mathbf{k}$ in the previous equation must yield 0:

\[\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{p}_1,\cdots,\mathbf{p}_n) \times a \sum_i {\color{red}{g_i}} \frac{\mathbf{p}_i \mathbf{k} \mathbf{p}_i}{2 \mathbf{k} \mathbf{p}_i} = \mathcal{A} \times a \sum_i {\color{red}{g_i}} \mathbf{p}_i = 0.\]

But the sum over all momenta is already zero by momentum conservation. Any linear combination of momenta weighted by couplings $g_i$ will not vanish, unless the $g_i$ are the same for all $i$: a massless spin 2 boson enforces universal interactions (the equivalence principle).

Therefore, for low momenta, any massless spin 1 particle can only interact with a coupling proportional to conserved charges. Moreover, any massless spin 2 boson can only interact with a universal coupling. That is why a photon corresponds to a massless spin 1 particle and the graviton to a massless spin 2 particle.

One can go further and do the same calculation for spin 3 or higher. This requires polarisation tensors with more indices. The resulting condition can never be fulfilled unless all couplings $g_i = 0$, which explains why there are no massless spin 3 particles or higher in QFT.
